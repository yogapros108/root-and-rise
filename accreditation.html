Accreditation

Standards that actually mean something

Most accreditation bodies inspect. We designed a system where quality is built in — through high standards, transparency, and feedback loops that reward excellence.

Anyone can call themselves an accreditation body. The question is: do the standards actually protect teachers, students, and the profession?


We built YogaPros because we saw standards dropping — courses getting shorter, contact hours disappearing, lead trainers with less and less experience. We believe yoga teachers deserve the same professional infrastructure as any other profession. That starts with accreditation you can trust.

Our approach

Two models of quality assurance

Traditional model

Inspect and police

Some people say we should send assessors to every course to inspect standards. But think about what that actually means.

  • Financially unviable — sending assessors to courses in 100+ countries would require enormous fees, passed on to trainers and students
  • Professionally insulting — a Senior Yoga Teacher with 8,000+ hours would not accept an individual assessor questioning their professional judgement on whether to pass or fail a graduate
  • Episodic — checked once, forgotten until renewal
  • Gameable — schools prepare for inspection day, then revert
  • Adversarial — creates an us-vs-them dynamic between body and trainer
  • No feedback loop — poor outcomes stay invisible between inspections
YogaPros model

Design for quality outcomes

We set the bar high at the gate, then create systems where quality is continuous, transparent, and self-correcting.

  • Continuous — quality signals flowing all the time
  • Transparent — graduate reviews visible to everyone
  • Collaborative — trainers and graduates grow together
  • Self-correcting — poor outcomes surface naturally
How it works

Seven mechanisms that protect standards



1

The highest gatekeeper standards in the industry

The lead trainer on any YogaPros-accredited course must be a Senior Yoga Teacher — minimum 8 years and 4,000 hours of teaching experience. They must personally deliver at least 70% of the 180 minimum contact hours.

This single requirement eliminates the biggest risk in teacher training: inexperienced trainers producing unready graduates.

Compare: Yoga Alliance USA requires just 4 years / 2,000 hours for lead trainers, and allows "Other Faculty" (non-registered trainers) to teach without equivalent experience requirements.

2

Verified graduate reviews

Every graduate from a YogaPros-accredited course can leave a review of their training — they don't need to join YogaPros to do this. We verify that the reviewer actually completed the course.

Five questions, but two matter most:

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

"Do you feel confident and ready to teach?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

"Would you work with this trainer again?"

A trainer who consistently produces graduates who don't feel ready to teach becomes visible — no inspector needed. The reviews are public and build over time on the trainer's Brand Profile.

Self-correcting — quality surfaces naturally
3

Pre-course assessment

Every YogaPros-accredited training school must run an effective pre-course assessment — ideally live and in person. This serves two purposes:

For the trainer: Ensure the right students are accepted on the course — the right level of practice, the right attitude, and realistic expectations.

For the student: A chance to meet the trainer, experience their teaching style, and assess whether this course is the right fit — before committing time and money.

Two-way assessment means better fit, fewer dropouts, and higher-quality outcomes for everyone.

4

First-year graduate support

The training environment and the real world are very different. A controlled classroom with supportive peers is nothing like walking into a studio full of strangers with injuries, expectations, and questions you didn't cover in training.

We ask trainers to support their graduate teachers through their first year of teaching. This is a win for everyone:

New Teacher

Real-world guidance when they need it most. Confidence to teach safely from day one.

Their Students

A better-prepared teacher who has ongoing support and mentorship behind them.

The Trainer

Real-world data on what new teachers actually face. Keeps courses relevant and up to date.

The trainer can offer additional training (paid or free) to address real-world gaps, update their course content based on actual feedback, and build a lifelong relationship with their graduate teachers. Graduates become raving fans — and leave more reviews on the trainer's Brand Profile and Live CV.

5

Practice requirements

We recommend all trainees have a minimum of two years of strong, regular practice before entering teacher training. You should be able to teach from experience — meaning you can demonstrate postures to an acceptable level for teaching.

This isn't about being the most flexible person in the room. It's about having enough embodied experience to teach safely, adapt to different bodies, and know the difference between a posture that works and one that doesn't.

6

Annual trainer reviews

Most accreditation bodies do a one-off check — a snapshot in time — then leave trainers alone until renewal. We review every trainer account and their events annually, checking for changes in standards, delivery, and compliance.

This isn't an inspection. It's an ongoing relationship. Combined with continuous graduate reviews, it means quality is monitored year-round — not just on the day someone applies for accreditation.

Continuous oversight — not a snapshot in time
7

Non-registered trainer requirements

We recognise that yoga teacher training benefits from specialist knowledge — anatomy, physiology, business, mental health. Non-yoga specialists can teach up to 30% of the course content.

But they must have a minimum of 4 years and 2,000 hours of experience in their chosen field. The remaining 70% or more must be delivered by the Senior Yoga Teacher lead trainer.

Compare: Yoga Alliance USA allows "Other Faculty" (non-registered trainers) to teach on courses with no equivalent experience requirement.

Development Training

Further training for qualified yoga teachers

Foundation training creates new teachers. Development training grows existing ones. These are qualified professionals continuing their education — the standards reflect that distinction, just as they do in medicine, law, and every other profession.

A clear distinction on pre-recorded content

Foundation Training

Pre-recorded content: never accepted. You cannot learn to teach yoga from a video. Foundation training requires 180 minimum contact hours with a Senior Yoga Teacher who has 8,000+ hours of experience. There are no shortcuts to becoming a qualified teacher.

Development Training

Pre-recorded content: accepted for qualified teachers. A qualified professional can learn new material through pre-recorded content — just as a doctor watches pre-recorded lectures for CPD. The difference: assessment is always live (in person or via Zoom). You prove competence face to face.

Regular Development

Continuing professional development

Further training for qualified yoga teachers to deepen their skills, expand their knowledge, and maintain their professional edge.

  • Minimum 28 hours total
  • Pre-recorded content accepted
  • Assessment must be live (in person or via Zoom)
  • Lead trainer: EYT or equivalent (min 4yrs / 2,000hrs)
  • For existing qualified yoga teachers only
Specialist Development

Higher-risk specialist training

Training in specialist categories that require hands-on competence. These carry higher risk and earn a verified specialty badge on your Live CV.

  • Minimum 28 contact hours, 40 total hours
  • Must be delivered live (in person or online live)
  • No pre-recorded content — the risk demands it
  • Lead trainer: EYT or equivalent (min 4yrs / 2,000hrs in topic)
  • Earns a verified specialty badge on your Live CV
  • Only qualified teachers can claim these specialties
