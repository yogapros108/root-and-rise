Foundation Accredited Training

What this badge means for you

You're about to invest serious time, money, and heart into becoming a yoga teacher. This badge tells you the course you're considering has been held to the highest training standards in the global yoga industry. Here's exactly what that means — and why it matters for your career from day one.

The problem

Not all yoga teacher training is created equal

There are thousands of yoga teacher training courses in the world. Some are life-changing. Some are weekend workshops dressed up as professional qualifications. From the outside, they look almost identical.

Same promises. Same glossy photos. Same "200-hour" label. But behind that label, the differences are enormous — in the experience of who's teaching you, how many hours are actually face-to-face, whether anyone checks if you're ready to teach, and what happens after you graduate.

The YogaPros Foundation Accredited Training badge exists so you don't have to guess. It means the course has been independently verified against the highest standards in the industry — standards that protect you as a student, and set you up for a professional career, not just a certificate.

What you get

Six things this badge guarantees

Every one of these is independently verified by YogaPros — the global professional body for yoga teachers. Not claimed by the trainer. Verified by the industry.

01

Your lead trainer has 8+ years and 4,000+ hours of teaching experience

They must hold Senior Yoga Teacher status — the highest professional tier. This isn't someone who did a training course last year and started teaching others. This is a deeply experienced professional who has taught thousands of hours across years of real-world practice.

Industry comparison: Yoga Alliance USA requires just 4 years / 2,000 hours for lead trainers. YogaPros requires double.
02

Your lead trainer personally delivers at least 70% of the course

The person whose name is on the course actually teaches it. Not assistants. Not guest teachers filling time. At least 70% of all contact hours are delivered by the Senior Yoga Teacher who designed and leads the training. You learn from the expert, not from their delegates.

Industry comparison: Yoga Alliance USA has no minimum percentage requirement for lead trainer delivery.
03

Minimum 180 contact hours — real teaching, not videos

Contact hours mean face-to-face learning with a qualified trainer — in person or live online. Not pre-recorded videos you watch alone. Not self-study modules. Real, interactive, responsive training where your trainer can see you, correct you, and guide you in real time.

Industry comparison: Yoga Alliance USA requires only 30 synchronous hours out of 200 total. Up to 85% can be pre-recorded. YogaPros: zero pre-recorded content. Ever.
04

Pre-course assessment — the course is the right fit for you

Before you commit your time and money, the trainer runs a proper assessment — ideally live, in person. This is two-way: the trainer checks you're at the right level, and you get to meet them, experience their teaching style, and decide if this is the right course for you.

This protects you from committing to a course that isn't right — before you've paid in full.
05

First-year support after graduation

The training environment and the real world are very different. A controlled classroom with supportive peers is nothing like walking into a studio full of strangers with injuries, expectations, and questions you didn't cover in training. YogaPros-accredited trainers are asked to support you through your first year of teaching — real-world guidance when you need it most.

Most courses end at graduation. This one continues into your career.
06

Any non-yoga specialists must have 4+ years / 2,000+ hours in their field

A Foundation course may include specialist teachers — anatomy, physiology, business, mental health. But they can only deliver up to 30% of the course, and they must have a minimum of 4 years and 2,000 hours of experience in their specific field. No fillers. No unknowns.

Industry comparison: Yoga Alliance USA allows "Other Faculty" (non-registered trainers) with no equivalent experience requirement.

Behind the badge

Seven quality mechanisms that protect you

1

The highest gatekeeper standards in the industry

The lead trainer must be a Senior Yoga Teacher — minimum 8 years and 4,000 hours of teaching experience. They must personally deliver at least 70% of the 180 minimum contact hours. This single requirement eliminates the biggest risk in teacher training: inexperienced trainers producing unready graduates.

Compare: Yoga Alliance USA requires just 4 years / 2,000 hours for lead trainers, and allows "Other Faculty" to teach without equivalent experience requirements.
2

Verified graduate reviews

Every graduate can leave a verified review — they don't need to join YogaPros to do this. We verify that the reviewer actually completed the course. Five questions, but two matter most: "Do you feel confident and ready to teach?" and "Would you work with this trainer again?" These are public and build over time.

3

Pre-course assessment

Every accredited school must run an effective pre-course assessment — ideally live and in person. For you: a chance to meet the trainer, experience their teaching style, and assess whether this course is the right fit — before committing time and money. For the trainer: ensuring the right students are accepted at the right level.

4

First-year graduate support

The training environment and the real world are very different. A controlled classroom with supportive peers is nothing like walking into a studio full of strangers with injuries, expectations, and questions you didn't cover in training. Trainers are asked to support graduates through their first year — real-world guidance when you need it most.

5

Practice requirements

We recommend all trainees have a minimum of two years of strong, regular practice before entering teacher training. You should be able to demonstrate postures to an acceptable level for teaching. This isn't about being the most flexible person in the room — it's about having enough embodied experience to teach safely.

6

Annual trainer reviews

Most accreditation bodies do a one-off check — a snapshot in time — then leave trainers alone until renewal. YogaPros reviews every trainer account and their events annually, checking for changes in standards, delivery, and compliance. Combined with continuous graduate reviews, quality is monitored year-round — not just the day someone applies.

7

Non-registered trainer requirements

Specialist teachers (anatomy, physiology, business, mental health) can teach up to 30% of the course. But they must have a minimum of 4 years and 2,000 hours of experience in their chosen field. The remaining 70%+ is delivered by the Senior Yoga Teacher lead trainer.

Compare: Yoga Alliance USA allows "Other Faculty" (non-registered trainers) to teach on courses with no equivalent experience requirement.

Before you choose

Questions to ask any training course

If a course carries the YogaPros Foundation badge, you already know the answers to all of these. If it doesn't, ask them — and see what happens.

Ask this

"How many years has the lead trainer been teaching?"

YogaPros accredited: minimum 8 years / 4,000 hours. If they can't tell you, or the number is low, that's a signal.

Ask this

"What percentage of the course does the lead trainer personally deliver?"

YogaPros accredited: minimum 70%. If they dodge this question, you're paying for the name but being taught by someone else.

Ask this

"Can I speak to past graduates independently?"

YogaPros accredited: yes — you can DM any reviewer directly through the platform. If they only offer hand-picked testimonials, ask why.

Ask this

"How many hours are pre-recorded vs live?"

YogaPros accredited: zero pre-recorded content. 180 minimum live contact hours. If they say "flexible" or "self-paced," dig deeper.

Ask this

"What support do you offer after I graduate?"

YogaPros accredited: first-year support recommended. If the answer is "here's your certificate, good luck" — you deserve better.

Ask this

"Is there a pre-course assessment?"

YogaPros accredited: required, ideally live. If they'll take anyone with a credit card, the course isn't selective — and neither is the qualification.
